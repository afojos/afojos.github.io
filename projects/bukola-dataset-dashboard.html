Income pattern observations
⚠️
Income dropped sharply in Q4
October, November and December each brought in only ₦6M — exactly half the ₦11.7M monthly average. This likely indicates seasonal work or a contract end. In those months, housing alone consumed over 100% of monthly income — savings built earlier in the year covered the gap.
RED FLAG
✅
March and August were outlier income months
March (₦18M) and August (₦19.1M) were significantly above the regular average. These likely represent bonuses or additional income. Without these two months, the annual total would have been ₦116.5M and the savings rate would have dropped from 32.8% to approximately 18.8% — below the 20% benchmark.
INSIGHT
💡
100% active income — no passive streams visible
Every credit in this dataset is a paycheck. No dividend payments, rental income, interest credits or freelance receipts appear. Full active income dependency means if paychecks stopped, cash flow would go negative within one month at current spending levels.

The 6 key findings
🏠
Finding 01 — Housing consumed 39.3% of all spending
Mortgage & Rent at ₦37.1M for the year is the dominant expense at 26.4% of gross income. The safe benchmark is 28–30% of take-home income on housing. Bukola is right at the ceiling. The Q4 income drop to ₦6M/month meant housing alone consumed over 100% of monthly income in those months — the savings built earlier in the year covered the shortfall.
CRITICAL
🔨
Finding 02 — Home Improvement was 6.4× over annual budget
Annual budget was ₦4.5M. Actual spending came in at ₦28.6M — a variance of ₦24.1M. This single category accounts for 30.3% of all 2018 spending and is the reason May and June went into deficit. Without this spend, the annual savings rate would have jumped from 32.8% to approximately 58%.
CRITICAL
🍽️
Finding 03 — Total food spending was 4.8× the grocery budget
The monthly grocery budget was ₦225,000. Actual average was ₦349,400 — 55% over. Add restaurants (₦3.9M), fast food (₦496K), and coffee (₦173K) and total annual food spending reaches ₦8.6M, or ₦716,800/month. Eating out alone cost more annually than the entire grocery budget.
WARNING
📱
Finding 04 — Digital bills exceeded budget by over 100% combined
Mobile phone at ₦2.52M vs ₦1.17M budget = 115% over. Internet at ₦2.36M vs ₦1.35M budget = 75% over. These are fixed recurring monthly bills yet they consistently exceed budget — indicating either the budget was set wrong or plan upgrades were never reflected in the budget.
WARNING
💰
Finding 05 — Savings rate of 32.8% is above the 20% benchmark
The internationally recognised minimum savings rate is 20% of income. Bukola's 32.8% is comfortably above this. However, this is heavily supported by two high-income months. Stripping out the renovation spend, the underlying monthly cash flow management in normal months was actually very disciplined — spending consistently between ₦5.7M and ₦7.3M on a ₦12M income.
STRENGTH
📊
Finding 06 — 15 of 19 budget categories were overspent
Only Alcohol & Bars, Television, Coffee Shops, and Entertainment came in under budget. Every other tracked category exceeded its annual budget. A well-functioning budget should have roughly 50–60% of categories at or below target. At 79% over budget, this is a budget that exists on paper but is not actively constraining behaviour.
ANALYSIS
Recommendations
🎯
Build a renovation reserve fund
Set aside ₦300,000–₦500,000/month into a dedicated home maintenance fund. When a major project hits, draw from the fund rather than the main cash flow. This eliminates the deficit months entirely.
🍔
Cap restaurant spending at grocery-level
Restaurants (₦3.9M) cost almost as much as groceries (₦4.2M) for the full year. Setting a monthly restaurant limit of ₦150,000 and tracking it would save approximately ₦2.2M annually.
📋
Update the budget to match reality
A budget where 79% of categories are consistently over target is not being used. Revise mobile phone and internet budgets to actual costs, then focus discipline on genuinely controllable categories.
📉
Plan for the Q4 income dip
If Q4 consistently brings lower income, spending in those months needs a corresponding plan. October through December were actually the best-managed months in terms of spending control.
