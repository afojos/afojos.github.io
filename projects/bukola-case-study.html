Overview

What This Dataset Is — And Why I Used It

My first project — the Naira Moves Personal Finance Audit — used my own four months of financial data. That proved the framework worked on one person. To show the methodology is repeatable on any dataset, I needed to apply it to completely external data from a different earner in a different context.

I found this dataset published by Bukola Fatunde on Kaggle — 806 real personal finance transactions covering a full calendar year. The data was in USD. I converted every figure at ₦1,500 per dollar and ran it through the exact same Naira Moves lens I built for my own audit. A separate Budget.csv file with 19 category targets made a budget vs actual comparison possible — one of the most powerful analyses you can run on personal spending data.

Methodology

How I Did the Analysis — Step by Step

The raw CSV had 806 rows across six columns: Date, Description, Amount, Transaction Type, Category, and Account Name. Here is exactly what I did in Excel to go from raw data to finished findings.

1
Data cleaning — removed noise, flagged exclusions
Two transaction types in the data are not real spending: Credit Card Payment (143 rows — just money moving between accounts, not actual spending) and Paycheck credits (46 rows — income, not expenses). I added an Exclude column using =IF(OR(E2="Credit Card Payment",E2="Paycheck"),"Exclude","Include"). This left 617 genuine spending transactions for analysis. Without this step, totals would have been wildly inflated by double-counting.
2
Currency conversion — every dollar multiplied by ₦1,500
Added an Amount (₦) column with =C2*1500 applied to all 806 rows. The ₦1,500 rate reflects the 2024 Nigerian market rate — a deliberate modelling choice to show what these spending patterns look like at today's exchange rate. This is not the 2018 rate. It is a framework exercise, not a historical conversion.
3
Income extraction — SUMIFS to isolate Paycheck credits by month
Income was not a separate sheet or column — it lived inside the transaction data labelled as Category="Paycheck" and Transaction Type="credit". I used =SUMIFS(amount,category,"Paycheck",type,"credit",month,M) for each of the 12 months. Total annual income: ₦140,625,000 across 46 paycheck entries. The income was not consistent — ranging from ₦6M in Q4 to ₦19.1M in August.
4
Spending by category — SUMIF across all 20 categories
Built a spending summary table using =SUMIF(category,cat_name,amount_col) for each of the 20 spending categories. Calculated each as a % of total spending and % of total income. This immediately exposed the two dominant categories — Mortgage & Rent (39.3%) and Home Improvement (30.3%) — consuming nearly 70% of all outflows combined.
5
Budget vs actual — compared against the Budget.csv file
The dataset included a separate Budget.csv with monthly targets for 19 categories. I multiplied each monthly budget by 12 for an annual figure, then calculated the variance (actual minus budget) for each category. Result: 14 of 19 budgeted categories were overspent. Home Improvement alone was over by ₦24.1M — more than five times the annual budget for that single category.
6
Monthly trends — income vs spending, net retained, savings rate
Built a 12-row monthly summary table showing income, spending, net retained, and savings rate for each month. This revealed the May–June anomaly — two consecutive months where spending (₦19M each) nearly equalled or exceeded income. Every other month averaged just ₦5.8M spending, a healthy 48% of income. The renovation was the single distorting event of the entire year.

Key Findings

6 Things the Data Revealed

🏠
Mortgage consumed 39.3% of all spending — above the danger threshold
At ₦37.1M for the year, housing costs represented nearly 26% of gross income. The globally accepted ceiling is 28–30% of take-home pay. At 39.3% of all spending, housing was the single dominant pressure point. Any income disruption — a missed paycheck, a slow month — would immediately create financial distress because housing is a fixed, non-negotiable cost. The ₦17.3M overspend vs the annual mortgage budget (₦19.8M budgeted, ₦37.1M actual) suggests the mortgage payment increased during the year — possibly an adjustable rate repricing.
Red Flag
🔨
Home Improvement ran 6.4× over annual budget — ₦28.6M vs ₦4.5M planned
This is the single most important finding in the entire dataset. The annual budget for home improvement was ₦4.5M (₦375K per month). Actual spend was ₦28.6M — an overage of ₦24.1M. This did not happen gradually. May (₦19.1M spend) and June (₦19.5M spend) were extreme outliers — spending in both months nearly equalled or exceeded monthly income of ₦12M. The renovation was funded almost entirely by drawing down savings accumulated from January to April. Without those strong early months, this earner would have gone into debt. This is the most common reason high-income earners end a year worse than expected — one unplanned capital project that was never properly budgeted.
6.4× Over
🍽️
Total food spending reached ₦9.7M — consistently over budget across 4 sub-categories
Groceries (₦4.2M vs ₦2.7M budget), Restaurants (₦3.9M vs ₦2.7M budget), Fast Food (₦496K vs ₦270K), Coffee Shops (₦173K — actually under at ₦270K budget). Combined food-related spend was ₦9.7M — 10.3% of all spending. No single meal is expensive. But every monthly restaurant visit and every grocery run compounded to ₦1.5M over budget on groceries alone by December. This is lifestyle creep in its most common form — small daily decisions that individually feel harmless but accumulate to a significant annual overspend.
₦9.7M Food
📱
Mobile Phone + Internet combined ₦4.9M — ₦2.36M over budget
Mobile Phone: ₦2.52M actual vs ₦1.17M budget (+₦1.35M over). Internet: ₦2.36M actual vs ₦1.35M budget (+₦1M over). Together these two digital connectivity categories ran ₦2.36M over budget annually. This mirrors the data bundle overspend pattern in my own Naira Moves audit — digital connectivity is systematically underbudgeted by earners who depend heavily on it for work and lifestyle, regardless of which country they are in.
₦2.36M Over
✅
Savings rate of 32.8% — above the 20% benchmark, but the renovation nearly cancelled it
₦46.1M net retained against ₦140.6M income. On the surface this is strong — well above the 20% minimum target. However, the May–June renovation cost ₦38.7M and was funded from savings built earlier in the year. Without the renovation, the year would have ended with approximately ₦84.7M retained — a savings rate closer to 60%. The savings discipline was real. But one unplanned capital expenditure consumed most of it. This is why emergency reserves and renovation budgets are not optional planning tools — they are the difference between a 32% and a 60% savings rate.
32.8%
📅
May + June = 41% of the entire year's spending in just two months
May: ₦19.1M spent. June: ₦19.5M spent. Combined ₦38.7M — which is 41% of the full annual outflow of ₦94.6M. Both months had income of only ₦12M each. The renovation was almost entirely financed from the savings surplus accumulated across January to April. Every other month of the year averaged just ₦5.8M in spending — a healthy, well-controlled 48% of income. The conclusion is clear: the underlying spending behaviour of this earner is disciplined. The renovation is the anomaly — and the lesson is that one unplanned project can undo an entire year of financial progress in 60 days.

Both earners spend 63–67% of income — the pattern repeats across countries
Different countries. Different currencies. Different income levels separated by a factor of 175. Yet the expense ratio came back within 4 percentage points of each other — 63% vs 67.2%. This is not a coincidence. It reflects a structural pattern in how earners relate to money: income tends to set the ceiling for lifestyle, and lifestyle tends to consume the income available. This finding is what validates the Naira Moves framework as a universal tool — not just a Nigerian personal finance model.
Universal
💡
Higher income alone does not create better financial discipline
The US earner made ₦140M equivalent — 175 times my income during the audit period. Their expense ratio (67.2%) was actually worse than mine (63%). Their savings rate is better — but only because the absolute income is so much larger, not because spending was better controlled. This confirms the central argument of the Naira Moves framework: income growth without a spending structure just means bigger numbers and the same proportional problem.

What I Learned

Skills Demonstrated in This Project

Beyond the findings, this project was specifically designed to demonstrate three analyst capabilities that matter for a data portfolio.

📊
Working with real external data — not just my own numbers
Any analyst can describe their own situation. The skill that matters professionally is taking an unfamiliar dataset, cleaning it, understanding its structure, and producing credible findings from it. This project demonstrates exactly that — 806 rows of someone else's data, cleaned, categorised, and analysed without prior knowledge of the dataset.
🔗
Applying a consistent framework across different datasets
The Naira Moves framework was built for my personal audit. Applying it unchanged to an American dataset — and having the comparison reveal meaningful patterns — shows the framework is robust, not just self-serving. This is the analyst skill of framework design: building a methodology that generates insight regardless of who the subject is.
📝
Converting data into written findings, not just numbers
Numbers alone are not analysis. The 6 written findings in this project each explain what the number means, why it matters, what caused it, and what the correct response would be. That translation from raw ratio to actionable narrative is the skill that separates data analysts from data reporters.
