The Business Question

What does unplanned spending actually cost over 12 months?

Most people know they overspend sometimes. But they rarely see the full-year cost of a single unplanned expense — how much it undoes, how long recovery takes, and what the year would have looked like without it.

This project analyses 806 real personal finance transactions from a full calendar year to answer that question with actual numbers. The dataset is from a US-based earner, converted to Naira at ₦1,500 per dollar as a modelling exercise. The currency is not the point. The pattern is.

Key Findings

3 Insights That Actually Matter

💥
Finding 01 · The Core Problem
Two months of renovation spending consumed 41% of the entire year's budget
Critical
May and June each saw ₦19M in spending — on a ₦12M monthly income. That is ₦38.7M in 60 days, which is 41% of the full annual outflow of ₦94.6M. The renovation was not a gradual problem. It was a concentrated event that hit two specific months and consumed all the surplus built across January to April. Every other month averaged a healthy ₦5.8M in spending — a 48% expense ratio. The earner was disciplined for 10 months and it still was not enough, because one event was never planned for.
May: ₦19.1M spent  ·  June: ₦19.5M spent  ·  Both months on ₦12M income
📋
Finding 02 · The Budget Gap
14 of 19 budgeted categories were overspent — but one category did 90% of the damage
Watch
Home Improvement was budgeted at ₦4.5M for the year. Actual spend: ₦28.6M. That single overage of ₦24.1M accounts for over 90% of the gap between what was planned and what was spent. Remove it, and the budget adherence score goes from 21/100 to above 80/100. The other 13 over-budget categories — Groceries (+₦1.5M), Restaurants (+₦1.2M), Mobile Phone (+₦1.35M) — are real problems, but they are lifestyle creep. The renovation was a capital expenditure with no capital budget. Those are two completely different problems with two different solutions.
Home Improvement: ₦4.5M budget → ₦28.6M actual  ·  6.4× over
🌍
Finding 03 · The Universal Pattern
A Nigerian earner and a US earner both spent 63–67% of income — same pattern, different currencies
Insight
When I ran this external dataset through the same framework I built for my own Nigerian personal finance audit, the expense ratios came back at 63% (mine) and 67.2% (this dataset). The US earner made ₦140M equivalent — 175 times my income. Yet the structural pattern was almost identical. This is not an income problem. It is not a Nigerian problem. Earners tend to spend roughly what they earn, regardless of how much they earn, unless there is a deliberate structure stopping them. That is the whole reason the Naira Moves framework exists.
My audit: 63% expense ratio  ·  Bukola dataset: 67.2%  ·  Different country, same problem
Recommendations

What Should Actually Change

Findings without recommendations are just observations. Here is what this earner — or any earner in a similar position — should do with this information.

01
Create a separate Capital Expenditure budget — completely separate from monthly expenses
The renovation was not a lifestyle expense. It was a capital project. Treating it as a monthly expense category is why it ran 6.4× over budget — monthly budgets are not designed to absorb ₦28M projects. The fix is to create a dedicated Capital Reserve account, contribute ₦500K–₦800K per month into it specifically for home improvement, vehicle replacement, and major one-time purchases. When the fund is not large enough, the project waits. This single structural change would have prevented the entire May–June crisis.
Impact: Would have kept spending within budget in 10 of 12 months
02
Build a 3-month emergency reserve before the next large capital project
The renovation was funded by drawing down savings accumulated from January to April. That means the earner entered the second half of the year with their buffer depleted. Any income disruption in July onwards would have created an immediate crisis. The rule is simple: never start a major capital project unless you have 3 months of average spending (approximately ₦17M–₦18M in this case) sitting untouched as an emergency reserve. The project starts only when the reserve is fully funded and separate from the project budget.
Impact: Protects against income disruption during and after a major expense
03
Address the housing cost — at 39.3% of spending, this is the structural risk
Mortgage and Rent consumed ₦37.1M for the year — 39.3% of all spending, well above the recommended 28–30% ceiling. This is not a cash flow problem, it is a structural one. Housing costs are fixed — they cannot be reduced month by month the way food or entertainment can. The earner has three options: renegotiate the mortgage terms to reduce the monthly obligation, increase income to bring the housing percentage below 30%, or accept the elevated risk and build a larger emergency reserve to compensate. Doing nothing leaves the household one disrupted month away from defaulting on the most important fixed cost.
Impact: Reduces structural financial risk — the one that cannot be managed in real time
